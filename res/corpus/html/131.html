WINTER OF DISCONTENT

When is the Qatar World Cup 2022 and how will the Premier League be affected? Here’s what we know

Football's showpiece will be switched to winter for the first time in its 86-year history in hugely controversial decision

  • Jamie Gordon
  • 6 Jun 2017, 15:39
  • Updated: 6 Jun 2017, 15:42

FIFA'S decision to award Qatar the 2022 World Cup has been slammed from all sides.

One of the main concerns was the soaring summer heat which led to the extraordinary decision to move the tournament to winter.

The switch will have a huge impact on European football, with the Premier League in particular as there is no winter break.

And the Gulf state has received further criticism after a number of Muslim countries broke off diplomatic relations over Qatar's alleged support of Isis.

Find out how the landmark decision could wreak havoc on the English top flight below.

When is the Qatar World Cup 2022?

Qatar's World Cup will take place from November 21 to December 18.

The date of the final has been selected to fall on the Middle Eastern country's national day.

Summer temperatures can reach 50C  which resulted in the tournament being moved from its traditional June-July berth for the first time ever.

Despite the winter dates, it could still reach 30C during the event and is unlikely to dip below 15C - even at night.

How will the Premier League be affected by the Qatar World Cup?

Losing 28 days from the calendar right in the middle of the season will pose huge risks for European football chiefs.

However most leagues have a two-week winter break which could be used to help soften the blow.

The Premier League does not have this option and could begin a month early in July with a slight extension to the end of the season also.

Players will need to be released for pre-tournament training with their international team-mates which will hit schedules further.

In addition, the struggles of integrating players tired from tournament football into their teams will be a huge issue for clubs.

Aaron Ramsey's injury following his exploits with Wales at Euro 2016 early this season highlights the risk meaning the issues could drag on well after the World Cup finishes.

How will the Qatar World Cup be different from other tournaments?

Aside from the date, awarding the tournament to Qatar has thrown up several other issues.

There have been alleged human rights abuses in the building on stadia and infrastructure.

In addition the sharp drop in oil prices has led to a dip in government spending, with organisers asking Fifa for permission to cut two or three venues from the proposed 12.

Qatar has strict rules on public consumption of alcohol and there may be a shortage of hotel rooms for fans.

Its national team are frankly downright poor and interest does not run high with matches poorly attended - leading to questions over the World Cup's legacy.

Treatment of women and homosexuals in the country have also been criticised.
