The winners and losers from the fourth Democratic debate

Updated Oct 16, 2019;Posted Oct 16, 2019
By Jonathan D. Salant | NJ Advance Media for NJ.com

U.S. Sen. Cory Booker of New Jersey had delivered three strong debate performances, but has failed to move up in the polls. Would the fourth time be a charm?

Meanwhile, U.S. Sen. Elizabeth Warren of Massachusetts has been surging in the polls, even surpassing former Vice President Joe Biden in some recent surveys. Did she maintain her momentum?

What about the others on a debate stage that featured 12 Democratic presidential candidates?

NJ Advance Media asked Democratic strategists and other experts to weigh in on Wednesday’s fourth presidential debate. Here’s what they had to say.

Democratic consultant Peter Fenn:

Winners:

Mayor Pete Buttigieg is more and more comfortable in the arena and showed it tonight, especially on Syria and his health plans. He is quick on his feet and sounds fresh.

Warren took hits but still is growing as a candidate and must show there is flexibility in her Medicare for all plan that keeps private insurance in place.

Biden showed more passion and anger toward Trump. His message of the anti-Trump, safer candidate came through. But can he energize the grass roots?

Amy Klobuchar continues to get stronger but will it translate into money, organizers and improved poll numbers?

Bernie Sanders was stronger than expected after his health scare and will get the endorsement of AOC in a rally later this week.

Losers:

Tulsi Gabbard did not seem to belong on the stage.

Julian Castro is still not measuring up.

Tom Steyer has to pay for it and doesn’t seem relevant as we approach Iowa.

Kamala Harris didn’t improve her standing much either, nor did Beto O’Rourke.

Monmouth University Polling Institute Director Patrick Murray:

Winners:

Warren was the main target of the night, which means her opponent think of her as the biggest threat. She parried those attacks well enough not to suffer any damage.

Buttigieg also seemed to find a voice that sets himself up as a viable alternative for voters who is not firmly committed to one of the front runners.

Andrew Yang was also a winner mainly for the fact that his proposals are becoming a central part of the conversation.

Booker and Klobuchar deserve mention too. They had really good performances – like they always do – but it’s still not likely to move the needle for either of them.

Sanders is also a winner for bouncing back from his heart attack and bringing the “full Bernie” to the debate stage.

Losers:

Harris has been fading and really needed to get back in the game, but she didn’t quite get there.

Biden did not necessarily do badly in this debate, but he wasn’t the center of attention given the news of the past two weeks, and that hurt him here.

Also, he wasn’t particularly strong on the Ukraine question he should have been prepared for. Everyone else – O’Rourke, Castro, Gabbard, Steyer – should think about packing it up.

Democratic consultant Julie Roginsky:

Winners:

1. Elizabeth Warren, who parried attacks all night long and came away unscathed.

2. Bernie Sanders, who assuaged any fears his supporters may have had about his health.

3. Pete Buttigieg, who took a risk being aggressive and who established himself as a leading contender from the moderate wing of the party.

Losers:

1. Joe Biden: He did nothing to assuage concerns about his readiness to take on Donald Trump in a general election debate. His performance wasn’t lethal by any means but it wasn’t reassuring.

2. Kamala Harris: She badly needed a breakout moment and she didn’t get one.

Krista Jenkins, political science professor at Fairleigh Dickinson University and poll director:

The symbiotic nature of debates and public opinion polls was on full display last night.

Biden stood for incrementalism, and those who flanked him – Warren and Sanders – pushed hard for a vision that’s anything but incremental. Nowhere was this more evident in the debate over health care. In a situation like this, there are no such things as winners and losers, as there are too many dimensions to a debate performance that the public evaluates to boil it down to such simplistic terms.

Warren took the biggest hits as everyone tried to chip away at the comprehensive nature of her policy positions. She weathered the hits and showed everyone that she’s capable of combing progressive values with personal anecdotes to defend policy positions that set her apart from everyone except for Sanders. Of course, it didn’t hurt that so much time was given to her and others who look good in polls.

As for others, despite spending millions of his own money to get the public on board for impeaching the president, it didn’t do much to buy Tom Steyer air time in a debate.

Here’s what new organizations had to say.

CNN:

Winners:

Pete Buttigieg. The South Bend mayor had one clear goal in the debate: Hit Elizabeth Warren on her support for “Medicare for All,” and make sure Democratic voters knew he had an alternate plan that would not eliminate the private health insurance market. Mission accomplished.

Andrew Yang. If I told you even three months ago that there would be a time in mid-October in which there was an extended conversation in a Democratic debate about the dangers of automation, you would have laughed at me.

Amy Klobuchar. At the moment, the Minnesota senator hasn’t qualified for the next debate in November. Knowing that, Tuesday night was her last best chance to make a real move. And to her immense credit, she went for it.

Bernie Sanders. If you were watching the debate to see how Sanders fared in the wake of his recent heart attack, well, he was the same old Bernie: Irascible, impatient, sneaky funny and entirely unapologetic about his liberal solutions to the problems facing the country.

Losers

Elizabeth Warren. Welcome to being the frontrunner! Although polling suggests that Warren and Joe Biden are co-front-runners, it was crystal clear Tuesday night that the other 11 candidates on stage viewed the Massachusetts senator as the top dog. Which, in theory, is a good thing for her! But in practice, it didn’t work out well.

Joe Biden. Biden needed a performance that wasn’t just “good for Biden” but good by any measure. He came close-ish but, to my mind, didn’t do enough.

Kamala Harris. Nothing Harris did on Tuesday night will change her trajectory -- downward in this race.

Tom Steyer. When the most interesting thing about you in the debate is that you wore a plaid Christmas tie, you didn’t have a good debate.

The Washington Post:

Winners:

Elizabeth Warren. For the first time in one of these debates, Warren found herself under sustained attack.

Bernie Sanders. In his first debate after suffering a heart attack, he was arguably more lively than he has been at any previous debate.

Amy Klobuchar. The Minnesota senator arguably made herself Warren’s chief foil in this debate, creating contrasts on taxes, Medicare-for-all and other issues.

Andrew Yang. He makes this list solely because some of his top issues — automation and universal basic income — got a real debate

Losers:

Joe Biden. Not only does he seem to no longer be the candidate his opponents fear most, but he was again somewhat off his game.

Tom Steyer. There are no second chances at first impressions, and Steyer — a billionaire whom Democrats have accused of buying his way on to the debate stage — didn’t do anything in the debate to overcome that.
